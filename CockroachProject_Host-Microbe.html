The cockroach project: community stability and dynamics in the gut of the American cockroach

The cockroach project explores the microbiome stability and dynamics in the hindgut of the American cockroach, Periplaneta americana. We use the cockroach as our model host organism because it shares striking similarities in both gut morphology and microbial community composition with humans and other mammals, while requiring far less maintenance and resources than mammalian models. Unlike results from diet studies in mice ( Turnbaugh et al. 2009, Faith et al. 2011 ), we have found that the 16S profile of the cockroach hindgut remains stable when the host is subjected to extreme dietary shifts (Tinker & Ottesen, 2016). Understanding communities' stability in the face of perturbation

Current research topics we are pursuing in this project include:

Defining the cockroach gut microbiome

A clearer picture of the phylogenetic and functional diversity of the cockroach gut microbiota is necessary to understand its resistance to perturbations. We have characterized the communities of our insects with 16S rRNA gene sequencing, but these sequences only provide so much information about the microbial community members. Sequencing single-cell genomes, metagenomes, metatranscriptomes, and cultured organisms from the gut increases our knowledge of potential microbe-microbe or host-microbe interactions that impact microbiome stability and resilience.

Gnotobiotic P. americana

We have established a system for producing and maintaining cockroaches with germ-free guts in the lab, enabling us to conduct experiments featuring gnoto- and xeno-biotic insects. These models can be used to study the assembly of synthetic or complex microbial communities.

Social behavior and transmission of microbiota

Living in colonies, cockroaches are gregarious insects whose coprophagic habits enable horizontal transmission of microbiota via feces. We use this co-housing phenomenon to observe community progression and turnover. By co-housing gnotobiotic insects with those carrying a standard lab cockroach community, we can observe how the microbiota are transmitted between individuals.

Nitrogen balance and in community stability

Metabolic waste nitrogen is generally excreted as urea, ammonia, or uric acid. In humans, uric acid accumulation leads to a painful condition called gout; cockroaches, however, purposefully store uric acid within specialized cells for utilization during times of nitrogen depletion. We are currently investigating the role of host uric acid stores in maintaining microbial community stability during periods of nutritional scarcity.
