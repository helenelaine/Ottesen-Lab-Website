The water project: assembly and function of microbial communities in the river networks of Athens

Streams are the link between terrestrial and marine environments and as so, they represent an important resource and habitat for a broad range of wildlife including microorganisms. Stream microbial communities play a main role in biochemical cycles and nutrient cycling. Our lab aims for a better understanding of microbes and microbial communities in stream environments. Located close to our lab facilities in Athens, GA, the Upper Oconee Watershed Network recruits and trains citizen scientists to assist in sample collection. In collaboration with community volunteers, we have been studying this stream system for more than five consecutive years. Using molecular tools including metagenomics, metatranscriptomics, 16S rRNA sequencing and single-cell genomics our lab has been able to contribute to the knowledge of this microbial community. Our strategy is directed to answer questions like: How the community composition shifts through the stream? Who are responsible for essential functions on the stream? Which biochemical processes are happening at different locations of the stream?
